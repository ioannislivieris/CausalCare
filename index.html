Research question
What is the causal effect of health insurance coverage (insured vs. uninsured) on total annual medical expenditure among U.S. working-age adults (25–64)? Using data from the Medical Expenditure Panel Survey (MEPS) 2022, we apply causal machine learning to isolate the true effect of coverage from confounding factors such as age, income, and health status — producing individual-level Conditional Average Treatment Effect (CATE) estimates that vary across patient profiles.

Positivity ✅
Age restriction to 25–64, propensity score trimming at e(x) ∈ [0.05, 0.95], and IPW weighting achieved SMD < 0.1 for all 13 covariates.
Unconfoundedness ✅
Placebo test ratio of 661x confirms genuine causal signal. E-value of 3.37 indicates an unmeasured confounder would need RR ≥ 3.37 to fully explain away the effect.
SUTVA ✅
No interference assumption is plausible at individual level. Treatment is defined as coverage as distributed in the 2022 U.S. population — a policy-relevant estimand.
Consistency ✅
Satisfied conditional on the policy-relevant estimand definition. Observed outcomes equal potential outcomes under the well-defined treatment.
Policy recommendations
What the numbers mean in practice
Health insurance increases access to and use of healthcare services by approximately $400 per person per year — translating to more visits, treatments, and care, especially for those currently avoiding care due to cost.
Is the effect the same for everyone?
The model shows a broadly positive effect across the population without strong individualization, meaning that expanding insurance coverage will have a generally beneficial impact without needing to target specific subgroups.
Who benefits most?
Models with better heterogeneity capture (Random Forest, C-XGBoost) suggest that individuals with poorer health status and lower income are likely to benefit more, though the top model does not clearly delineate specific subgroups.
Population-level impact
Expanding coverage to the uninsured (5.5% of the population) is expected to substantially increase total access to care — reducing cost-driven avoidance and improving population health outcomes.
Data · MEPS 2022
The model was trained on the Medical Expenditure Panel Survey (MEPS) 2022, a nationally representative survey of U.S. civilian non-institutionalized adults conducted by AHRQ. The outcome variable (TOTEXP22) was winsorized at the 99th percentile ($93,768) and log-transformed (log1p) to address skewness. The analytic sample covers working-age adults aged 25–64 after positivity enforcement.
